Psychology Behind: A Silent Voice

An emotional dive into guilt, trauma, and the power of listening through the award-winning anime film.

June 10, 2025
12 min read
Nafisa Ahmed

A Silent Voice might seem like a gentle, romantic slice-of-life animation film at first, but it peels off into a traumatic story like an onion. Beneath its soothing serene animated scenes, it orchestrates a raw, unflinching dive into guilt, trauma, and the scars we all might relate to at some point in life.

A Film That Transcends Time

Directed by Naoko Yamada, this 2016 anime isn't just a movie—it's an emotional gut punch. The story revolves around Shoya Ishida and Shoko Nishimiya, two high schoolers whose lives are tangled together by a past filled with cruelty and regret.

Nishimiya, a deaf girl, whose only desire is to just fit in. Alas, her disability bounds her into a closeted barrier from others in elementary school. Ishida, who's desperate for validation, impulsively joins in tormenting her, oblivious to the damage he's causing. Although the bullying eventually ended, the physical and emotional damage perpetuated.

A shocking parallel: Ishida becomes the next target of isolation and bullying. His friends slowly begin to abandon him and he shuts himself up from the world as guilt, fear and dejection shackles his soul and mind.

Grey Characters and Moral Complexity

What makes A Silent Voice so immersive and realistic is its "grey character" and the complex emotions it evokes. The film doesn't present clear villains or heroes—instead, it shows us flawed human beings struggling with guilt, forgiveness, and the desire for connection.

The Psychological Depth of Trauma

It is self-evident that Ishida's actions stem from passive-aggressive behavior influenced by peers, social norms, and a desperate need to fit in or be validated. In school environments, following the 'tides' is quite a common pattern where dominant groups single out an individual to reinforce social hierarchies that children perceive from adults.

Scapegoating Psychology:

A study by the National Center of Education Statistics highlights how a lack of inclusivity and acknowledgment about being different or disabled results in skyrocketed bullying. The infamous incident shifted the tide from Nishimiya to Ishida as he became the 'scapegoat'.

This is the most thought-provoking aspect of Silent Voice as we behold the breakthrough of scapegoating and mob mentality. Although everyone in the class played a role in the bullying scandal, Ishida was thrown off the ship as the only bad guy.

Scapegoating is a well-versed concept done by Baumeister and Leary in 1995 where one person becomes the bearer of collective guilt. The mob mentality where individuals follow the dominant behavior even though it is wrong or unethical as the social tides shift, a concept introduced by Doris is still evident and common among everything including from children's classrooms to political teams.

Secondary Trauma in Families

The movie also unfolds the bitter aftermath of depression. Nishimiya's family dynamics add another layer to her struggle—Her younger sister, Yuzuru, shows dissociative behaviors, such as photographing dead animals, as a coping mechanism, as she views her sister losing the 'essence of life.'

This reflects the psychological concept of secondary trauma, where family members of individuals with disabilities or psychological disorders also experience an emotional strain.

The Twisted Cycle of Guilt and Redemption

A rare syndrome among depressed patients which often gets buried in society is guilt. In Nishimiya's case, instead of resenting her tormentors, she spirals into blaming herself. In one scene, she scrubs Ishida's vandalized desk—tears trickling down her face and her trembling body resembling the inner turmoil as she continues erasing the evidence that she is the "cause".

Her train of thought process can be seen in her inner monologues: "What if I didn't enroll here? I am the reason everything fell apart. Everyone especially Ishida would be better off if I were dead."

Self-Directed Blame:

This is a psychological response called "self-directed blame", where individuals believe they are the cause of their own suffering and even the suffering of others. This self-blame is tragically common among bullying victims.

They internalize every bad outcome in life, creating a narration where they villainize themselves. Nishimiya's pain is a stark reminder of how deeply words and actions can cut, even if it wasn't directed at her.

Moral Injury and Self-Isolation

Guilt is an infernal poison—one drop it slowly spreads and eats away at you, long after the damage is done. In the film, Ishida embodies this perfectly. Since that incident, he withdrew himself from the world, cornering himself in the darkness by covering people's faces with X marks because he couldn't bear to look them in the eye.

He has convinced himself that he doesn't deserve any interaction or connection—a result of having prolonged low self-esteem. Psychologists call this type of response moral injury—a deep, unmanageable struggle to reconcile one's past actions with one's present self. Ishida's journey isn't just about earning Nishimiya's forgiveness, it's about forgiving himself as well.

According to the American Psychological Association, this internalized self-blame and chronic depressive thoughts can lead to low self-esteem, chronic anxiety, and even suicidal ideation.

The Turning Point—A Life Saved, A New Beginning

One of the major emotionally intense moments in A Silent Voice is the bridge confrontation, where Ishida is forced to face his past alongside the very friends who turned on him. The scene screams volumes by its rawness, of the raging emotions, in each character making it the first breakthrough contradiction with the film's initial commence on serene, introspective imagery.

This moment captures the psychological turmoil of unresolved conflicts as he specifies how each of his friends is not as nice or good as they think they are. His words like "You don't even know me and You only thought about your self-loving image" unleash their repressed emotions, we see how guilt, resentment, and misunderstanding have shaped their lives.

The anger, tears, and desperate arguments are strikingly realistic. It's rare to see an anime film tackle emotional breakdowns with such unfiltered authenticity. People aren't always ready to forgive. Trauma-infused pain is long-term pain no matter how tiny or simple or short it is.

The Suicide Attempt Scene

Then comes the most popular and insanely well-projected scene of Nishimiya's attempt to end her life which truly reinforces and emphasizes the drastic effect and eye-opening perception for both characters. Despite her warmth and the care she's shown to others, she's overwhelmed by a deep sense of self-hatred.

As she steps onto the balcony, the world around her falls into an eerie silence as if time itself has paused. The scene plays with dark colors and silent background noise that alludes to the situation. Instantly, Ishida reaches out and grabs her. But in a heartbreaking turn, he's the one who falls.

"Suicide doesn't stop the pain; it only passes it on to others."

His near-death experience becomes a pedestal shift of transformation—not just for him, but for everyone in his life. This scene is a powerful and unflinching reminder of how interconnected our struggles and healing can be.

The Power of Listening vs. Hearing

In the unforgettable final scenes, when Ishida wakes from his coma, we see Shoko drowning in guilt. It's the first time both characters reveal their most vulnerable selves. Ishida signing "friend" as he acknowledges his past actions sends ripples of emotion through the air.

This was the first time he "listened" to her and not just heard. Nishimiya struggled mostly because she never found someone who was willing to "listen" to her. That subtle yet one of the most fundamental differences is the biggest reason she led herself to the edge of despair and wanted to end it all.

You might be hearing. But are you listening?

This was a masterpiece projection of how depressed or lonely people feel when they speak—everyone around them only hears but doesn't listen. This is why this specific scene speaks volumes of emotional disparities, for they are finally face to face peeling out into their most true forms and actually communicating.

The Art of Visual Storytelling

Beyond its profound psychological messages, A Silent Voice stands out as an awe-inspiring visual and auditory wonder. Every sound effect, hues of color theory and camera work depicts and adds to the sensory aspect of the film.

For example—scenes shown through Nishimiya's eyes frequently feel nearly muted, suggesting how she perceives the world around her. This approach helps audiences to correlate her reality, evoking sympathy not through the spoken word but through silence.

Ishida's view of people with "X" marks over their faces is the most iconic tactic that illustrated his self-imposed social exile so well without any extra scenes or inner monologue. Finally, that moment when those "X" marks fall away carries an ineffable amount of strength and his way of accepting himself.

Healing, Inclusivity, and Acknowledgment

The ending of A Silent Voice proves that forgiveness doesn't erase the past, but it helps healing and hope to sprout. As Ishida walks into a school festival, finally lifting his head and for the first time the X marks that used to symbolize his isolation and loss of faith in humankind begins to fade.

The silence and for the first time the noises of people and children laughing, and customers bickering at the market all flood in. We can genuinely feel the surge of relief and happiness for him as he sobs at the fact he was able to free himself from his shackles which he used to believe was impossible for him.

This embodies the essence of inclusivity and how naturally magnificent human connection is. Nishimiya's struggles reflect the real-life challenges faced by people with disabilities, and the film urges us to do better—to see them, to include them, and to listen.

If this story resonates with you, take a moment to reflect: Who in your life might need to hear that they matter? Perhaps, is it yourself? Have you ever felt that prolonged awkwardness of being uncomfortable in your own skin?

In the end, A Silent Voice is not just a film. It reflects our flaws, our need for acceptance and the quiet battles so many of us fight within. It challenges us to be more than passive bystanders in the lives of others. Because sometimes, the most powerful voice is the one that trembles in silence, waiting for someone to truly listen.

So let's listen. And maybe, just maybe, in doing so, we'll help someone believe that they too are worthy of love, healing and a second chance.
