The Psychology behind the Award winning
Animation film- A Silent Voice
By Nafisa Ahmed
A Silent
Voice might seem like a gentle, romantic slice-of-life animation film at first, but it peels
off into a traumatic story like onion. Beneath its soothing serene animated scenes, it
orchestrates a raw, unflinching dive into guilt, trauma, and the scars we all might relate
to at some point in life.
A Film That Transcends Time
Directed by Naoko Yamada, this 2016 anime isn’t just a movie—it’s an emotional gut
punch. The story revolves around Shoya Ishida and Shoko Nishimiya, two high
schoolers whose lives are tangled together by a past filled with cruelty and regret.
Nishimiya, a deaf girl, whose only desire is to just fit in. Alas, her disability bounds her
into a closeted barrier from others in elementary school.  Ishida, who’s desperate for validation, impulsively joins in
tormenting her, oblivious to the damage he’s causing. Although the bullying eventually ended, but
the physical and emotional damage perpetuated. A shocking parallel:
Ishida becomes the next target of isolation and bullying. His friends slowly begin to
abandon him and he shuts himself up from the world as guilt, fear and dejection
shackles his soul and mind. What makes A Silent Voice so immersive and realistic is its “grey character” and
absence of a villain portrayal. Initially, Ishida’s actions come off as cruel, but they don’t
come from malice—they come from a desperate need to belong. Depression tangled with regret—an experience we may resonate with.
Initially, the audience develops a general loathing for Ishida, especially during the
infamous hearing aid incident (he accidentally pulled it out resulting in blood and fright in
the class) that ultimately becomes the turning point for both Shoko’s transfer and
Ishida’s journey of being ostracized. It is self-evident that Ishida’s actions stem from
passive-aggressive behavior influenced by peers, social norms, and a desperate
need to fit in or be validated. In school environments, following the ‘tides’ is quite a
common pattern where dominant groups single out an individual to reinforce social
hierarchies that children perceive from adults. A study by the National Center of
Education Statistics highlights howa lack of inclusivity and acknowledgment about being different or disabled results in skyrocketed bullying. Thus, the
infamous incident shifted the tide from Nishimiya to Ishida as he became the
‘scapegoat’. This is the most thought-provoking aspect of Silent Voice as we behold
the breakthrough of scapegoating and mob mentality. Although everyone in the class
played a role in the bullying scandal, Ishida was thrown off the ship as the only bad guy.
Scapegoating is a well-versed concept done by Baumeister and Leary in 1995 where
one person becomes the bearer of collective guilt. The mob mentality where individuals
follow the dominant behavior even though it is wrong or unethical as the social tides
shift, a concept introduced by Doris is still evident and common among everything
including from children's classrooms to political teams. The movie also unfolds the bitter aftermath of depression. Nishimya’s
family dynamics add another layer to her struggle—Her younger sister, Yuzuru, shows
dissociative behaviors, such as photographing dead animals, as a coping mechanism, as she views her sister losing the ‘essence of life.’.
This reflects the psychological concept of secondary trauma, where family members of
individuals with disabilities or psychological disorders also experience an emotional
strain.
The Twisted Cycle of Guilt and Redemption
A rare syndrome among depressed patients which often gets buried in society
is guilt. In Nishimya’s case, instead of resenting her tormentors, she spirals into blaming
herself. In one scene, she scrubs Ishida’s vandalized desk— tears trickling down her face and her trembling body resembling the inner turmoil as she continues erasing the
evidence that she is the ”cause”. Her train of thought process can be seen in her inner monologues-
“What if I didn’t enroll here? I am the reason everything fell apart. Everyone especially
Ishida would be better off if I were dead.” This is a psychological response called
"self-directed blame", where individuals believe they are the cause of their own
suffering and even the suffering of others.
This self-blame is tragically common among bullying victims. They internalize every bad
outcome in life, creating a narration where they villainize themselves. Nishimiya’s pain is
a stark reminder of how deeply words and actions can cut, even if it wasn’t directed at
her.
Guilt is an infernal poison-one drop it slowly spreads and eats away at you, long after
the damage is done. In the film, Ishida embodies this perfectly. Since that incident, he
withdrew himself from the world, cornering himself in the darkness by covering people’s
faces with X marks because he couldn’t bear to look them in the eye. He has convinced
himself that he doesn’t deserve any interaction or connection- a result of having
prolonged low self-esteem. Psychologists call this type of response moral injury—a
deep, unmanageable struggle to reconcile one’s past actions with one’s present self.
Ishida’s journey isn’t just about earning Nishimiya’s forgiveness, it’s about forgiving
himself as well.
When a friend drifts away, we usually assume it’s because we weren’t “good
enough. It’s an ingrained human response, one that rips your
self-worth apart mirroring that of an invisible monster. According to the American Psychological Association, this internalized
self-blame and chronic depressive thoughts can lead to low self-esteem, chronic
anxiety, and even suicidal ideation.The Turning Point—A Life Saved, A New Beginning
One of the major emotionally intense moments in A Silent Voice is the bridge
confrontation, where Ishida is forced to face his past alongside the very friends who
turned on him. The scene screams volumes by its rawness, of the raging emotions, in each character making it the first breakthrough contradiction with the film’s initial
commence on serene, introspective imagery.
This moment captures the psychological turmoil of unresolved conflicts as he specifies
how each of his friends is not as nice or good as they think they are. His words like “You
don’t even know me and You only thought about your self-loving image” unleash their
repressed emotions, we see how guilt, resentment, and misunderstanding have shaped
their lives. Ishida’s former friends—who once abandoned him—express their own
frustrations, revealing that trauma inflicts everyone involved in different ways.
The anger, tears, and desperate arguments are strikingly realistic. It’s rare to see an
anime film tackle emotional breakdowns with such unfiltered authenticity. People aren’t
always ready to forgive. Trauma-infused pain is long-term pain no matter how tiny or
simple or short it is. So is the fact that apologies don’t always fix what’s broken but only
provide closure to recover and move on.
This confrontation marks the start of the plot-thickening, showing us that healing thrives
on honesty, no matter how tough the truth might be and this was something that was
encouraged for us to do as well, instead of living in a masked world - hiding your utmost
feeling even from your loved ones.
Then comes the most popular and insanely well-projected scene of Nishimiya’s attempt
to end her life which truly reinforces and emphasizes the drastic effect and eye-opening
perception for both characters. Despite her warmth and the care she’s shown to others,
She's overwhelmed by a deep sense of self-hatred. As she steps onto the balcony, the
the world around her falls into an eerie silence as if time itself has paused. The scene plays
with dark colors and silent background noise that alludes to the situation. Instantly,
Ishida reaches out and grabs her. The animation in this moment does a splendid job of
capturing the depth of her anguish and surprise as Ishida, who has been haunted by her
because he lost his friends twice (and supposedly should be happy if she
dies-according to her), chooses to save her. But in a heartbreaking turn, he’s the one
who falls. His near-death experience becomes a pedestal shift of transformation—not
just for him, but for everyone in his life. This scene is a powerful and unflinching
reminder: suicide doesn’t stop the pain; it only passes it on to others. The raw and
moving portrayal of how interconnected our struggles and healing can be is
mind blowing to see in the movie industry in general.
Lastly, in the unforgettable final scenes, when Ishida wakes from his coma, we see
Shoko drowning in guilt. It’s the first time both characters reveal their most vulnerable
selves. Ishida signing “friend” as he acknowledges his past actions sends ripples of
emotion through the air. Meanwhile, Shoko lets go of her bottled-up feelings through
sign language, illuminating the beauty of their authentic connection. Their heartfelt exchange becomes the ultimate healing balm, allowing them to release their burdens
and guilt. This was the first time he “listened” to her and not just heard. Nishimiya
struggled mostly because she never found someone who was willing to “listen” to her.
That subtle yet one of the most fundamental differences is the biggest reason she led
herself to the edge of despair and wanted to end it all.
You might be hearing. But are you listening?
This is what was portrayed when Ishida learned sign language to no longer have the
barrier of communication. It was the best reconciliation step but what good was it if he
never “listened” to her? Him, brushing off her numerous attempts to speak out her
emotions heavily inflicted further pain and trauma. This was a masterpiece projection of
how depressed or lonely people feel when they speak—everyone around them only
hears but doesn’t listen. This is why
this specific scene speaks volumes of emotional disparities, for they are finally face to
face peeling out into their most true forms and actually communicating.
The Art of contrast between the plot and animation
Beyond its profound psychological messages, A Silent Voice stands out as an
awe-spiring visual and auditory wonder. Every sound effect, hues of color theory and
camera work depicts and adds to the sensory aspect of the film.
For example-scenes shown through Nishimiya's eyes frequently feel nearly muted,
suggesting how she perceives the world around her. This approach helps audiences to
correlate her reality, evoking sympathy not through the spoken word but through
silence. Even in the last scene, we see a sudden firework lit up after they officially
reconciles-adding up to that euphoric moment.
Such an approach invites audiences to experience her reality, fostering empathy not
through dialogue, but through the absence of sound. Water emerges as a recurring
theme, embodying the emotions that the characters struggle to communicate. Subtle
hints like koi fish swimming gracefully and humming reflections rippling on river
surfaces, play both the calm and chaos of the protagonists’ minds.
Ishida’s view of people with “X” marks over their faces is the most iconic tactic that
illustrated his self-imposed social exile so well without any extra scenes or inner
monologue.Finally, that moment when those "X" marks fall away carries an ineffable amount of
strength and his way of accepting himself. This artistic mix enhances A Silent Voice
beyond a simple story into a visual masterpiece with an unspoken psychological aspect
of life where even silence establishes such powerful plotlines.
Healing, Inclusivity, and Acknowledgment
The ending of A Silent Voice proves that forgiveness doesn’t erase the past, but it helps
healing and hope to sprout. As Ishida walks into a school festival, finally lifting his head
and for the first time the X marks that used to symbolize his isolation and loss of faith in
humankind begins to fade. The silence and for the first time the noises of people and
children laughing, and customers bickering at the market all flood in. We can genuinely
feel the surge of relief and happiness for him as he sobs at the fact he was able to free
himself from his shackles which he used to believe was impossible for him. This
embodies the essence of inclusivity and how naturally magnificent human connection is.
Nishimiya’s struggles reflect the real-life challenges faced by people with disabilities,
and the film urges us to do better—to see them, to include them, and to listen.
If this story resonates with you, take a moment to reflect: Who in your life might need to
hear that they matter? Perhaps, is it yourself? Have you ever felt that prolonged
awkwardness of being uncomfortable in your own skin?

In the end, A Silent Voice is not just a film. It reflects our flaws, our need for acceptance and the quiet battles so many of us fight within. It challenges us to be more than passive bystanders in the lives of others. Because sometimes, the most powerful voice is the one that trembles in silence, waiting for someone to truly listen.
So let’s listen. And maybe, just maybe, in doing so, we’ll help someone believe that they too are worthy of love, healing and a second chance.
